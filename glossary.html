Payments Glossary

Plain-English definitions, acronyms, and support-focused explanations for common payment processing terms.

A

🏦 Acquiring Bank / Acquirer

The merchant-side financial institution, or processor acting on its behalf, that supports card acceptance and helps move funds to the merchant.

Why It Matters: Acquirers are involved in merchant accounts, funding, disputes, and processing risk.

Related: Processor • Merchant Account • Settlement • Funding

✅ Authorization

A real-time request asking the issuing bank whether a transaction should be approved or declined.

Remember: Authorization does not mean the merchant has been paid.

Related: Capture • Decline Code • Issuer

🏠 AVS

Address Verification Service checks the numeric parts of a billing address or ZIP code against the issuing bank’s records.

You’ll See This When: A card-not-present transaction is declined or flagged because the billing information does not match.

Related: CVV • Card Not Present • Fraud Settings

B

📦 Batch

A group of approved and captured transactions submitted together for clearing and settlement, usually at the end of the business day.

You’ll Use This When: A merchant says they were not funded or their deposit is missing.

Related: Settlement • Funding • Capture

C

🧾 Capture

The merchant’s official claim on an approved authorization. Without capture, the transaction does not move forward into the batch.

Example: A restaurant may authorize the card first, then capture the final amount after the tip is added.

Related: Authorization • Batch • Settlement

💳 Cardholder

The person or business authorized to use a credit or debit card.

Related: Issuer • Card Network • Authorization

🌐 Card Network / Card Brand

The network that routes transaction data between acquirers and issuers and sets operating rules. Examples include Visa, Mastercard, American Express, and Discover.

Why It Matters: Card networks set rules for interchange, disputes, authorization, and settlement.

Related: Issuer • Acquirer • Interchange

🏪 Card Present

A transaction where the physical card or mobile wallet is presented in person. Examples include tap, dip, and swipe.

Related: EMV • NFC • Terminal

💻 Card Not Present

A transaction where the card is not physically presented, such as online, invoice, phone, or subscription payments.

Why It Matters: Card-not-present transactions usually carry higher fraud risk and rely more heavily on AVS, CVV, and gateway rules.

Related: AVS • CVV • Gateway • Virtual Terminal

⚠️ Chargeback

A formal reversal of a transaction initiated by the cardholder through their issuing bank.

You’ll See This When: A customer claims fraud, duplicate billing, missing goods, or dissatisfaction with a purchase.

Related: Dispute • Issuer • Reserve

🧹 Clearing

The exchange of finalized transaction data between the processor, card network, acquirer, and issuer so everyone can reconcile what is owed.

Related: Settlement • Batch • Interchange

🔐 CVV / CVC / CID

The 3- or 4-digit security code printed on the card, used to help verify that the customer has the card during card-not-present transactions.

Critical Rule: Merchants should never store CVV after authorization.

Related: AVS • PCI DSS • Card Not Present

D

🏷️ Descriptor

The business name or billing label that appears on a customer’s card statement.

Why It Matters: Confusing descriptors can lead to chargebacks because customers do not recognize the charge.

Related: Chargeback • Merchant Account

📉 Downgrade

A transaction that does not qualify for the best interchange rate because it missed required data, timing, or security criteria.

You’ll See This When: A merchant asks why processing costs are higher than expected.

Related: Interchange • Batch • Level 2/3 Data

E

💳 EMV

Chip-card technology that improves security for in-person card transactions and helps reduce counterfeit fraud.

Related: Terminal • Card Present • NFC

F

💰 Funding

The final deposit of settled card payment funds into the merchant’s business bank account.

Merchant Translation: This is when the money actually lands.

Related: Batch • Settlement • Reserve

⏳ Funding Delay

A delay between processing transactions and the merchant receiving funds. Common causes include weekends, holidays, unclosed batches, risk holds, or processor schedules.

Related: Batch • Funding • Reserve

G

🌉 Gateway

The digital equivalent of a payment terminal for e-commerce, virtual terminal, mobile app, or card-not-present payments. It securely captures and transmits transaction data to the processor.

Related: Virtual Terminal • Card Not Present • Processor

I

🏛️ Interchange

The wholesale fee paid to the issuing bank for accepting a card transaction. It is usually the largest part of a merchant’s processing cost.

Why It Matters: Interchange varies based on card type, industry, transaction method, and data quality.

Related: Downgrade • Card Network • Settlement

🏦 Issuing Bank / Issuer

The cardholder’s bank. It issues the card and decides whether to approve or decline authorization requests.

Support Tip: If the issuer declines a transaction, the processor usually cannot override it.

Related: Authorization • Decline Code • Cardholder

M

🏪 Merchant Account

A specialized account used to accept and settle card payment funds before they are deposited into the merchant’s bank account.

Related: Acquirer • MID • Funding

🆔 MID

Merchant Identification Number. A unique identifier assigned to a merchant account by the acquirer or processor.

You’ll Use This For: Merchant lookup, equipment programming, funding research, and chargeback investigation.

Related: TID • Merchant Account • Processor

N

📲 NFC

Near Field Communication, the wireless technology used for tap-to-pay cards and mobile wallets.

Related: EMV • Card Present • Terminal

P

🔒 P2PE

Point-to-Point Encryption encrypts card data inside the payment terminal immediately when the card is read.

Why It Matters: P2PE helps reduce PCI scope because the merchant’s local network does not handle readable card data.

Related: PCI DSS • Terminal • Tokenization

🛡️ PCI DSS

Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard. A set of security requirements for protecting cardholder data.

Related: SAQ • P2PE • Tokenization

🖥️ POS

Point of Sale. The hardware and software used to ring up items, calculate totals, and pass payment amounts to a terminal.

Related: Terminal • TID • Card Present

⚙️ Processor

The backend system or company that routes transaction data, supports authorization, manages batching, and helps with clearing and settlement.

Why It Matters: Merchants often contact the processor or ISO for funding, terminal, batch, and account support.

Related: Acquirer • Gateway • Settlement

R

↩️ Refund

A merchant-initiated return of funds to the customer after a transaction has settled.

Related: Void • Settlement • Chargeback

🏦 Reserve

Funds held back by a processor as protection against future chargebacks, fraud, or unpaid fees. A reserve may be rolling, capped, or fixed.

Related: Chargeback • Risk Hold • Funding

S

📝 SAQ

Self-Assessment Questionnaire. A PCI compliance form many merchants complete annually based on how they accept card payments.

Related: PCI DSS • P2PE • Tokenization

🏦 Settlement

The movement of funds between financial institutions after transactions are cleared.

Remember: Settlement is not the same as merchant funding.

Related: Clearing • Funding • Batch

T

📟 Terminal

A physical payment device used to accept card-present payments by tap, dip, or swipe.

Related: POS • TID • EMV

🔢 TID

Terminal Identification Number. A unique ID assigned to a specific terminal or gateway lane.

Example: One merchant can have one MID and many TIDs, such as a grocery store with many checkout lanes.

Related: MID • Terminal • POS

🔐 Tokenization

The process of replacing sensitive card numbers with a secure placeholder called a token.

Why Merchants Use It: Tokenization supports recurring billing and reduces the need to store card data.

Related: PCI DSS • P2PE • Recurring Billing

V

💻 Virtual Terminal

A secure web-based portal where merchants can manually enter card information for phone, mail, invoice, or card-not-present payments.

Related: Gateway • Card Not Present • AVS

🚫 Void

A cancellation of a transaction before it settles. Unlike a refund, a void stops the transaction before money officially moves.

Related: Refund • Settlement • Batch

☎️ Voice Authorization

A manual authorization obtained by calling an authorization center when electronic approval is unavailable or additional verification is required.

Related: Authorization • Decline Code • Issuer

PCI Compliance & Security

Every business that accepts credit cards has a responsibility to protect cardholder data. PCI DSS establishes the security standards that help merchants safeguard sensitive payment information.

What is PCI DSS?

The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS) is a global set of security standards designed to protect cardholder data.

Important: PCI DSS is not a government law. It is a contractual requirement developed by the PCI Security Standards Council and enforced through the payment ecosystem by card brands, acquiring banks, and payment processors.

Why It Matters Failure to maintain PCI compliance can result in fines, increased processing fees, forensic audits after a breach, or even the loss of the ability to accept card payments.

Merchant Levels

Who Has the Most Requirements?

Level Annual Volume Typical Requirement
1 Over 6 Million QSA Audit + Report on Compliance
2 1–6 Million Annual SAQ
3 20,000–1 Million E-Commerce Annual SAQ
4 Most Small Businesses Annual SAQ + Possible Quarterly Scans
Good to Know: Card brands may designate a merchant as Level 1 after a significant security breach regardless of transaction volume.

Self Assessment Questionnaires

Choosing the Right SAQ

The correct SAQ depends on how a merchant accepts payments—not simply how many payments they process.

  • SAQ A – Fully outsourced hosted payment pages.
  • SAQ A-EP – Merchant hosts the website, but payment data posts directly to the processor.
  • SAQ B-IP – Standalone IP-connected payment terminals.
  • SAQ D – Merchants that store, process, or transmit cardholder data directly.
Support Tip: Choosing the wrong SAQ can leave a merchant both non-compliant and unnecessarily burdened with additional requirements.

Implementation Best Practice

Reduce PCI Scope

Every implementation should aim to reduce the number of systems that handle cardholder data. Less scope means:

  • Less liability
  • Simpler PCI requirements
  • Lower security risk
  • Easier annual compliance

Tokenization

Sensitive card numbers are replaced with secure tokens while the processor stores the actual card data in a protected vault.

Point-to-Point Encryption (P2PE)

Certified payment terminals encrypt card data immediately after it is read, keeping the merchant's local network out of PCI scope.

Critical Security Rule

Never Store Sensitive Authentication Data

  • ❌ CVV / CVC codes
  • ❌ PIN numbers
  • ❌ Full magnetic stripe (track) data
Remember: Even if a customer gives permission, merchants cannot retain CVV values after authorization.

Support Insight

PCI Compliant ≠ PCI Certified

A payment gateway or software platform may be built to support PCI compliance, but merchants are still responsible for completing their annual SAQ and maintaining appropriate security controls.
